Overview

Heurist is a web service which captures and manages information about many different types of item - web bookmarks, bibliographic references, maps, people, events, images, projects, objects etc. (currently more than 60 item types - new ones are easily added). Personal notes, discussions, wiki pages, web links, images and geographic location can be added to any item in the database. Items can be cross-referenced.

Heurist allows one to easily and quickly find, capture and share relevant information within workgroups, organise information resources and generate teaching/research project web site content automatically (including within most Content Management Systems and teaching systems such as WebCT). No programming skills are required.

Some example web sites developed with content drawn from Heurist include:

  • University of Sydney Archaeology web site

  • EARTH - Early Agricultural Remains and Technical Heritage (ESF-funded network)

  • UNESCO Three Cities project web site

  • Rethinking Timelines ARC Linkage project

Full Description

Heurist is a free online database service which stores:

  • Internet bookmarks
  • Bibliographic references
  • Research data on other types of item such as descriptions of events, biographical records, sound recordings, photographs, historical maps, thesis topics, seminars etc. There are currently >60 record types and new ones are being added as people request them.

Heurist aims to put all of these records at your fingertips in a single system available anywhere, rather than as a multiplicity of systems tied to specific computers.

Some of the specific functions Heurist offers include:

  • Immediate access to your personal collection of bookmarks bibliographic records, research data and notes in a standard web browser anywhere you have access to the Internet.
  • Bookmark and tag (keyword) web pages and bibliographic references as you come across them, enter full bibliographic information (or import existing bibliographies or library searches).
  • Add private, workgroup or public annotations in the form of notes, discussion and wiki pages to any record in the database. You can also attach images and multimedia.
  • Create a personal home page which gives one-click access to all your most frequently used resources - add or remove them simply by adding or removing a keyword.
  • Rapidly search your collection by keyword or free text search to re-locate web sites, bibliographic entries and research notes.
  • Save complex searches with a user-defined name for re-use.
  • Print out or export any search you do in a variety of formats, including bibliographic formats (new formats can be added by writing a stylesheet).
  • Publish live search results to a web page (including web pages within the University CMS). Update the page simply by adding records to the database and keywording them to appear in a particular page.
  • Publish search results as live interactive maps in TimeMap, Google Maps or Google Earth.
  • Discover records added by others (if public) through social bookmarking (following tagging patterns) or keyword/text searches, and add them to your personal collection.
  • Send emails about one or several records to a person, or to all members of your workgroup(s), who can add these records to their personal collection with three clicks.
  • Set triggers on records so that they send you or your workgroup(s) email reminders on a specified date or at specififed intervals.
  • Make time-stamped connections between records in the database eg. connecting images or notes to bibliographic references. Has also been used for mapping a research network and for building a network of historical events.

Nearly ready

The following functions are under development and should be available in September 2007

  • Cite-As-You-Write function for Word
  • Synchronisation with Zotero (a browser-based academic bookmarking and bibliographic system from George Mason University)

For the more technical

  • Social bookmarking functions to discover new information from user tagging.
  • Generates RSS, GML and KML feeds from any specified search.
  • All searches generate a generic XML feed which is restyled with XSLT and Cocoon. New XSLTs can be added or referenced externally.
  • Wizard to build easily modified, complex PHP/MySQL online databases without programming. Uses same, or external, user authorisation database.
  • Job tracking and work hours tracking systems linked to same user authorisation database

Developers

Heurist was developed from late 2005 for the SHSSERI (Sydney Humanities and Social Sciences e-Research Initiative) Research Cluster by Dr Ian Johnson and staff of the Archaeological Computing Laboratory, University of Sydney.
